# Assessment Types Catalog

This reference catalogs authentic assessment types suitable for replacing or supplementing traditional assessments in higher education courses. Use this catalog when selecting an assessment format during the assessment-redesign process.

---

## Project-Based Assessments

**Description**: Students complete a substantial, multi-phase project that requires applying course concepts to solve a realistic problem. Projects typically span multiple weeks and involve planning, implementation, iteration, and reflection. Deliverables usually include both a product (code, design, report) and process documentation (design rationale, progress logs).

**When to Use**: Use project-based assessments when the course emphasizes application, synthesis, or creation. Appropriate for courses where professional practice involves building things -- software engineering, design, research methods, data analysis, and technical writing. Best suited as replacements for comprehensive exams or term papers.

**Strengths**: High authenticity and career relevance. Develops professional skills (project management, iterative development, technical communication) alongside domain knowledge. Allows assessment at higher Bloom's levels (Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create). Students report higher engagement and perceived learning value.

**Weaknesses**: Time-intensive to grade. Difficult to standardize across students/groups. Plagiarism detection is more complex than for exams. Scope creep can lead to workload imbalance. Group projects introduce free-rider problems.

**Example**: In a database systems course, students design and implement a database for a realistic business scenario. Deliverables: entity-relationship diagram, normalized schema, SQL implementation, sample queries with performance analysis, and a design rationale document.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create

**Typical Student Effort**: 20-60 hours depending on scope

**Typical Grading Effort**: 30-60 minutes per student (or per group)

## Portfolio Assessments

**Description**: Students compile a curated collection of work products over the semester that demonstrates their learning trajectory. Portfolios include selected artifacts (assignments, projects, reflections) accompanied by narrative commentary explaining what each artifact demonstrates and how the student's understanding has evolved.

**When to Use**: Use portfolio assessments when the course values growth over time, metacognitive reflection, and the ability to self-evaluate. Appropriate for courses with multiple smaller assignments that build on each other. Effective as replacements for final exams in courses emphasizing cumulative learning.

**Strengths**: Captures learning over time rather than at a single point. Develops metacognitive skills through reflection. Allows students to demonstrate their best work. Accommodates different learning speeds -- late bloomers can showcase final-state understanding. Supports multiple types of evidence.

**Weaknesses**: Requires clear portfolio structure guidelines or students produce unfocused collections. Reflection quality varies widely and is difficult to teach quickly. Grading rubrics must balance artifact quality with reflection quality. Initial setup time for faculty is significant.

**Example**: In an introductory programming course, students maintain a portfolio containing their five best coding assignments, each annotated with a reflection on what they learned, what challenges they faced, and what they would do differently. The portfolio concludes with a synthesis essay connecting course concepts to the student's career goals.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate

**Typical Student Effort**: 10-25 hours (cumulative across the semester, including reflection writing)

**Typical Grading Effort**: 20-40 minutes per student

## Performance Assessments (Demonstrations and Presentations)

**Description**: Students demonstrate their knowledge and skills through live or recorded performances: oral presentations, technical demonstrations, poster sessions, code walkthroughs, or teaching sessions. Performance assessments evaluate communication skills alongside domain knowledge.

**When to Use**: Use performance assessments when oral communication, professional presentation, or live problem-solving is a valued outcome. Appropriate for courses with WOC (Writing/Oral Communication) general education designations. Effective for capstone courses, seminars, and courses emphasizing professional readiness.

**Strengths**: Assesses communication skills that written assignments cannot. Reveals depth of understanding through Q&A. Mimics professional contexts (conference talks, client presentations, code reviews). Difficult to plagiarize. Provides immediate formative feedback opportunities.

**Weaknesses**: Time-intensive to schedule and attend (especially for large courses). Students with presentation anxiety may underperform relative to their knowledge. Grading in real-time is challenging -- consider recording for later review. Not suitable as sole assessment of technical depth.

**Example**: In a software engineering course, each team presents a 15-minute technical demo of their project to the class, followed by 10 minutes of Q&A. The presentation must cover the system architecture, key design decisions, challenges encountered, and a live demonstration of core functionality.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate

**Typical Student Effort**: 5-15 hours (preparation plus rehearsal)

**Typical Grading Effort**: 10-20 minutes per student (or per group, using a rubric during the presentation)

## Case Study Analysis

**Description**: Students analyze a detailed real-world or realistic scenario, identify problems, apply course concepts to diagnose causes, and propose solutions. Case studies present ambiguous, multi-faceted situations that require judgment and prioritization rather than a single correct answer.

**When to Use**: Use case study analysis when the course emphasizes analytical thinking, decision-making under uncertainty, and the application of theory to practice. Appropriate for courses in business, engineering ethics, project management, systems design, and policy analysis. Effective as replacements for traditional essay exams.

**Strengths**: Develops analytical and decision-making skills. Engages students with realistic complexity. Supports multiple valid approaches, rewarding critical thinking over memorization. Can be designed to embed ethical considerations naturally. Reusable across semesters with minor modifications.

**Weaknesses**: Writing good cases is time-intensive. Grading requires evaluating reasoning quality, not just correctness. Students unfamiliar with case-based learning may struggle initially. Cases can become outdated as technology or industry practices change.

**Example**: In a computer networks course, students receive a case study describing a company experiencing intermittent network failures. The case includes network topology diagrams, traffic logs, configuration files, and user complaints. Students must diagnose the root cause, propose a fix, estimate the cost and downtime of implementation, and justify their approach.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Analyze, Evaluate, Apply

**Typical Student Effort**: 8-20 hours depending on case complexity

**Typical Grading Effort**: 15-30 minutes per student

## Technical Design Documents

**Description**: Students produce a professional-grade design document for a system, algorithm, experiment, or process. The document follows industry or discipline conventions and includes problem analysis, proposed solution, alternatives considered, trade-off analysis, implementation plan, and risk assessment.

**When to Use**: Use technical design documents when the course values planning, systems thinking, and professional writing. Appropriate for upper-division and graduate courses in engineering, computer science, and the sciences. Effective as replacements for traditional research papers or as complements to implementation projects.

**Strengths**: Develops professional writing and planning skills. Requires students to think through a problem completely before implementing. Assesses understanding of trade-offs and constraints. Produces artifacts similar to professional deliverables. Pairs well with implementation projects (design doc first, then build).

**Weaknesses**: Students may lack familiarity with the format without explicit instruction. Quality depends heavily on writing skill, which may not be the primary learning outcome. Can feel disconnected from practice if not paired with implementation. Grading requires reading lengthy documents carefully.

**Example**: In an operating systems course, students write a design document for a custom process scheduler. The document includes: problem statement, requirements analysis, three candidate scheduling algorithms with trade-off analysis, detailed design of the selected approach, pseudocode, performance predictions, and a testing plan.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Analyze, Evaluate, Create

**Typical Student Effort**: 15-30 hours

**Typical Grading Effort**: 25-45 minutes per student

## Peer Teaching

**Description**: Students prepare and deliver a lesson on a course topic to their peers. The lesson may be delivered live, as a recorded video, or as written instructional materials. Students must demonstrate mastery of the content and the ability to explain it clearly to others.

**When to Use**: Use peer teaching when deep understanding and communication are valued outcomes. Appropriate for courses where explaining concepts is a professional skill (future educators, technical leads, consultants). Effective for reinforcing particularly challenging topics. Can be used as a replacement for quizzes or short exams on specific topics.

**Strengths**: Requires deep understanding -- students cannot teach what they do not understand. Develops communication and pedagogical skills. Benefits both the teacher (through preparation) and the audience (through peer explanation). Produces reusable learning resources.

**Weaknesses**: Quality varies significantly without scaffolding and clear rubrics. Students may propagate misconceptions if not monitored. Scheduling can be complex. Assessment must evaluate both content accuracy and teaching effectiveness. Not suitable for high-stakes summative assessment without supplementary measures.

**Example**: In a discrete mathematics course, pairs of students create a 20-minute video lesson on an assigned proof technique (induction, contradiction, contrapositive). The video must include a clear explanation of the technique, two worked examples of increasing difficulty, and a practice problem with solution. Peers evaluate the video using a provided rubric.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Understand, Apply, Evaluate, Create

**Typical Student Effort**: 8-15 hours (research, preparation, recording/delivery)

**Typical Grading Effort**: 15-25 minutes per student (or per pair)

## Reflective Journals

**Description**: Students maintain an ongoing journal documenting their learning process, challenges encountered, strategies attempted, connections to prior knowledge, and evolving understanding. Entries are typically short (300-500 words) and written at regular intervals (weekly or after each major topic).

**When to Use**: Use reflective journals when metacognition, self-regulated learning, and personal growth are valued outcomes. Appropriate for courses undergoing pedagogical change (to gather student feedback on new methods). Effective as a low-stakes complement to high-stakes assessments. Useful in experiential learning contexts (internships, service learning, labs).

**Strengths**: Develops metacognitive awareness and self-regulation skills. Provides ongoing insight into student thinking for the instructor. Low-stakes format reduces anxiety. Captures learning process, not just outcomes. Helps students identify their own knowledge gaps.

**Weaknesses**: Grading for depth and authenticity is subjective and time-consuming. Students may produce superficial entries without guided prompts. Not suitable as a sole assessment of technical knowledge. Requires consistent submission and feedback cycles to be effective.

**Example**: In an introduction to AI course, students write weekly journal entries responding to structured prompts: "What concept from this week was most challenging and why? How does this week's topic connect to last week's? How might a professional in your target career field use this concept?" The journal is assessed three times during the semester using a rubric evaluating reflection depth, conceptual accuracy, and growth over time.

**Bloom's Levels Assessed**: Understand, Analyze, Evaluate

**Typical Student Effort**: 1-2 hours per entry, 15-30 hours total across a semester

**Typical Grading Effort**: 5-10 minutes per entry per student (when graded periodically, not every entry)
