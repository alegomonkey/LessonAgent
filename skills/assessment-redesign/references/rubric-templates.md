# Rubric Templates

This reference provides rubric templates and guidelines for creating criterion-referenced rubrics during the assessment-redesign process. All rubrics use four performance levels: Exceeds Expectations, Meets Expectations, Developing, and Beginning.

---

## Generic 4-Level Criterion-Referenced Rubric Template

Use this template as a starting point for any rubric. Replace placeholder text with assessment-specific descriptors.

| Criterion | Weight | Exceeds Expectations (4) | Meets Expectations (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Criterion 1 Name] | [X%] | [Observable behavior demonstrating exceptional performance on this criterion. Specify what makes it exceed rather than merely meet.] | [Observable behavior demonstrating competent, satisfactory performance. This is the target level for most students.] | [Observable behavior demonstrating partial understanding or incomplete execution. Specify what is missing or insufficient.] | [Observable behavior demonstrating minimal engagement or fundamental misunderstanding. Specify what is absent or incorrect.] |
| [Criterion 2 Name] | [X%] | [Exceeds descriptor] | [Meets descriptor] | [Developing descriptor] | [Beginning descriptor] |
| [Criterion 3 Name] | [X%] | [Exceeds descriptor] | [Meets descriptor] | [Developing descriptor] | [Beginning descriptor] |
| [Criterion 4 Name] | [X%] | [Exceeds descriptor] | [Meets descriptor] | [Developing descriptor] | [Beginning descriptor] |

**Learning Outcome Mapping**: [List each criterion and the learning outcome(s) it assesses]

**Career Skill Annotations**: [List each criterion and the professional competency it develops]

**Scoring**: Total points = sum of (criterion score * criterion weight). Convert to percentage or letter grade according to course grading policy.

## Example Rubric: Technical Design Document

**Assessment**: Students produce a professional technical design document for a system, algorithm, or process. The document includes problem analysis, proposed solution, alternatives considered, trade-off analysis, and implementation plan.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed**:
- LO-3: Analyze a computing problem and identify appropriate solutions
- LO-5: Communicate effectively in written form about technical topics
- LO-7: Evaluate trade-offs among design alternatives

| Criterion | Weight | Exceeds Expectations (4) | Meets Expectations (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Problem Analysis** | 25% | Thoroughly identifies all relevant aspects of the problem including constraints, stakeholders, and edge cases. Frames the problem in terms that directly inform the solution space. Identifies non-obvious requirements. | Identifies the core aspects of the problem including primary constraints and stakeholders. Problem statement is clear and complete enough to guide solution design. | Identifies some aspects of the problem but misses important constraints or stakeholders. Problem statement is vague or incomplete in ways that affect downstream design. | Problem description is superficial or inaccurate. Missing critical constraints. Does not demonstrate understanding of what the system must accomplish. |
| **Solution Design** | 30% | Proposed solution is well-structured, addresses all identified requirements, and demonstrates sophisticated understanding of relevant concepts. Design decisions are explicitly justified with reference to requirements. Includes detailed specifications sufficient for implementation. | Proposed solution addresses core requirements and demonstrates solid understanding of relevant concepts. Major design decisions are justified. Specifications are sufficient for a competent implementer to proceed. | Proposed solution addresses some requirements but has gaps or inconsistencies. Some design decisions are unjustified or poorly reasoned. Specifications lack detail in important areas. | Proposed solution is incomplete, infeasible, or does not address the stated problem. Design decisions are absent or arbitrary. Specifications are insufficient for implementation. |
| **Evidence and Trade-off Analysis** | 25% | Presents three or more alternatives with rigorous comparison across multiple dimensions (performance, cost, complexity, maintainability). Uses quantitative evidence where applicable. Trade-off analysis leads logically to the selected solution. Acknowledges limitations of the chosen approach. | Presents at least two alternatives with meaningful comparison. Trade-off analysis covers the most important dimensions and supports the selection rationale. Some quantitative evidence is provided. | Presents alternatives but comparison is superficial or one-sided. Trade-off analysis misses important dimensions or is based on unsupported claims rather than evidence. | Does not present alternatives, or alternatives are trivially different. No meaningful trade-off analysis. Selection appears arbitrary. |
| **Communication Quality** | 20% | Document is exceptionally well-organized with clear section structure, consistent formatting, and professional tone. Writing is concise and precise. Diagrams and figures are effective and well-integrated. No significant grammatical or spelling errors. | Document is well-organized and readable. Section structure is logical. Writing is clear. Diagrams are present and useful. Minor grammatical or formatting issues do not impede understanding. | Document organization is inconsistent. Some sections are unclear or poorly structured. Writing is sometimes vague or imprecise. Diagrams are missing or unhelpful. Several grammatical or formatting issues. | Document is disorganized or difficult to follow. Writing is unclear, excessively verbose, or riddled with errors. No diagrams or entirely ineffective diagrams. Professional standards are not met. |

**Learning Outcome Mapping**:
- Problem Analysis --> LO-3 (Analyze a computing problem)
- Solution Design --> LO-3 (Identify appropriate solutions)
- Evidence and Trade-off Analysis --> LO-7 (Evaluate trade-offs)
- Communication Quality --> LO-5 (Communicate effectively in writing)

**Career Skill Annotations**:
- Problem Analysis --> Requirements Engineering, Systems Thinking
- Solution Design --> Technical Architecture, Engineering Design
- Evidence and Trade-off Analysis --> Data-Driven Decision Making, Critical Analysis
- Communication Quality --> Technical Writing, Professional Communication

## Example Rubric: Career-Branched Project

**Assessment**: Students complete a project aligned with their target career path. All variants assess the same learning outcomes through different professional lenses. This rubric applies to all career-path variants of the project.

**Learning Outcomes Assessed**:
- LO-1: Apply fundamental concepts to solve problems in the student's domain of interest
- LO-2: Implement a working solution using appropriate tools and techniques
- LO-4: Analyze results and draw evidence-based conclusions
- LO-5: Communicate technical work effectively
- LO-6: Demonstrate professional competency relevant to target career path

| Criterion | Weight | Exceeds Expectations (4) | Meets Expectations (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Technical Implementation** | 25% | Implementation is correct, efficient, and robust. Handles edge cases and error conditions. Code or artifacts are well-organized, documented, and follow discipline-appropriate conventions. Demonstrates mastery of relevant tools and techniques. | Implementation is correct for standard cases and reasonably organized. Code or artifacts follow basic conventions. Demonstrates competent use of relevant tools and techniques. Minor issues do not affect core functionality. | Implementation has significant bugs or gaps that affect core functionality. Organization is inconsistent. Tool usage demonstrates basic familiarity but not proficiency. Requires substantial revision to be functional. | Implementation is incomplete, non-functional, or fundamentally incorrect. Does not demonstrate understanding of the tools or techniques required. Cannot produce meaningful results. |
| **Domain Application** | 20% | Project demonstrates deep understanding of how the technical concepts apply in the chosen career domain. Problem selection is genuinely relevant to professional practice. Solution approach reflects awareness of domain-specific constraints and norms. | Project demonstrates adequate understanding of the career-domain connection. Problem is relevant to the field. Solution approach is reasonable for the domain, though it may miss some domain-specific nuances. | Connection to career domain is superficial or forced. Problem selection is only tangentially relevant. Solution approach does not reflect awareness of how professionals in the field actually work. | No meaningful connection to a career domain. Problem could apply to any field without modification. Domain application appears to be an afterthought rather than a design driver. |
| **Analysis Depth** | 20% | Analysis is thorough, well-reasoned, and draws on multiple sources of evidence. Conclusions are strongly supported by data or logical argument. Limitations are acknowledged and their implications discussed. Demonstrates original insight beyond what was covered in class. | Analysis addresses the key questions with adequate depth. Conclusions are supported by evidence. Major limitations are acknowledged. Demonstrates understanding of course concepts as applied to the project. | Analysis is present but shallow. Some conclusions are unsupported or weakly argued. Important limitations are overlooked. Demonstrates basic recall of concepts but limited analytical application. | Analysis is absent, perfunctory, or fundamentally flawed. Conclusions contradict the evidence or are stated without support. No awareness of limitations. Does not demonstrate analytical thinking. |
| **Documentation** | 15% | Documentation is comprehensive, well-structured, and sufficient for another practitioner to understand and reproduce the work. Includes clear methodology description, data sources, assumptions, and results. README or equivalent is professional-grade. | Documentation covers the essential elements: what was done, how, and why. Another practitioner could understand the work with minimal additional explanation. Organization is logical. | Documentation is incomplete or poorly organized. Key elements are missing (methodology, assumptions, or results). Another practitioner would need significant clarification to understand the work. | Documentation is absent or unusable. No meaningful description of methodology or results. Work cannot be understood or evaluated without direct explanation from the author. |
| **Presentation** | 20% | Presentation is compelling, well-rehearsed, and tailored to the audience. Clearly communicates the problem, approach, results, and significance. Visual aids are effective. Speaker handles questions confidently and accurately. Time management is precise. | Presentation communicates the essential information clearly. Visual aids are adequate. Speaker demonstrates knowledge of the work and can answer most questions. Time management is within acceptable bounds. | Presentation is unclear in places or poorly organized. Visual aids are missing or unhelpful. Speaker struggles with some questions or appears uncertain about aspects of the work. Time management is significantly off. | Presentation fails to communicate the work effectively. Speaker reads from slides or notes without engaging the audience. Cannot answer basic questions about the work. Significantly over or under time. |

**Learning Outcome Mapping**:
- Technical Implementation --> LO-2 (Implement a working solution)
- Domain Application --> LO-1 (Apply concepts to domain), LO-6 (Professional competency)
- Analysis Depth --> LO-4 (Analyze results, draw conclusions)
- Documentation --> LO-5 (Communicate technical work)
- Presentation --> LO-5 (Communicate technical work), LO-6 (Professional competency)

**Career Skill Annotations**:
- Technical Implementation --> Software Development, Technical Proficiency, Tool Mastery
- Domain Application --> Domain Expertise, Professional Awareness, Industry Context
- Analysis Depth --> Critical Thinking, Data Analysis, Evidence-Based Reasoning
- Documentation --> Technical Writing, Knowledge Management, Reproducibility
- Presentation --> Oral Communication, Audience Awareness, Professional Presence

## Rubric Design Guidelines

### Number of Criteria

Use 4 to 6 criteria per rubric. This range provides sufficient granularity to differentiate performance dimensions without overwhelming graders or obscuring the assessment's focus for students. If you find yourself with more than 6 criteria, consider whether some can be combined (e.g., "grammar" and "formatting" can become "communication quality"). If you have fewer than 4, consider whether important dimensions are being collapsed together inappropriately.

### Writing Level Descriptors

Follow these principles when writing the text for each performance level:

- **Use observable, measurable language.** Describe what the student's work looks like, not what the student understands internally. "Identifies three or more design alternatives" is observable. "Demonstrates deep understanding" is not.
- **Differentiate adjacent levels clearly.** A reader should be able to distinguish Meets from Developing without ambiguity. The difference should be qualitative (what is present or absent), not merely quantitative (more vs. less).
- **Anchor "Meets Expectations" first.** Define the target level before writing the others. Exceeds should describe performance that goes meaningfully beyond the target. Developing should describe performance that is on the right track but falls short. Beginning should describe performance that indicates fundamental gaps.
- **Avoid negative-only descriptors.** Even at the Beginning level, describe what the student did, not only what they failed to do. "Provides a problem statement that addresses surface-level symptoms without identifying root causes" is more informative than "Does not analyze the problem."
- **Keep descriptors parallel in structure.** Use the same grammatical structure across levels for each criterion. This makes comparison easier and grading faster.

### Mapping Criteria to Learning Outcomes

Every criterion must map to at least one learning outcome from the course syllabus. Document this mapping explicitly in the rubric. The mapping serves three purposes:

1. It ensures the assessment actually measures what it claims to measure (validity).
2. It provides evidence for accreditation reporting that specific outcomes are being assessed.
3. It helps students understand why each criterion matters.

If a criterion cannot be mapped to a learning outcome, either the criterion is measuring something not in the course objectives (remove it or add the outcome to the syllabus) or the mapping is indirect and should be made explicit.

### Annotating with Career Competencies

Add a career-skill annotation to each criterion indicating which professional competency the criterion develops. Use competency names that are recognizable across industries:

- Technical Communication
- Critical Thinking
- Systems Thinking
- Data-Driven Decision Making
- Project Management
- Collaboration
- Professional Ethics
- Domain Expertise
- Tool Mastery
- Problem Decomposition

These annotations help students see the professional value of academic work and provide data for tracking career-skill development across the curriculum. They also support the career-path branching feature by making explicit which professional skills each assessment variant emphasizes.

### Weighting Criteria

Distribute weights to reflect the relative importance of each criterion to the assessment's purpose:

- The criterion most closely aligned with the primary learning outcome should carry the most weight (typically 25-35%).
- No single criterion should carry more than 40% of the total weight (to prevent a single dimension from dominating).
- No criterion should carry less than 10% of the total weight (if it is worth less than 10%, it may not warrant its own criterion).
- Weights must sum to 100%.

Communicate weights to students before the assessment so they can allocate effort appropriately.
