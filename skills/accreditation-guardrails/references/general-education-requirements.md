# University of Maine General Education Requirements Reference

This reference documents the University of Maine General Education requirements relevant to the accreditation-guardrails skill. Use this when checking proposed course changes against general education compliance for courses carrying gen-ed designations.

---

## General Education Categories

The University of Maine General Education program requires students to complete courses across multiple categories. Courses carrying a gen-ed designation must satisfy specific outcome requirements and include signature assignments that are assessed and reported annually.

### WOC -- Writing and Oral Communication

**Full Outcome Text**: Students will communicate effectively through written and oral expression appropriate to a variety of audiences and purposes.

**Specific Requirements**:
- Courses must include at least one substantial writing assignment (minimum 1500 words of polished prose, or equivalent in technical writing).
- Courses must include at least one significant oral communication component (formal presentation, structured debate, oral defense, or equivalent).
- Both writing and oral communication must be assessed using rubrics aligned to the WOC outcome.
- Writing must involve a drafting and revision process, not just a single submission.

**Signature Assignment**: The designated signature assignment for WOC courses must assess both written and oral communication. The rubric must include criteria for clarity, organization, evidence use, audience awareness, and (for oral components) delivery effectiveness. The signature assignment must be submitted annually to the General Education assessment office with aggregate rubric scores.

**Changes Allowed Without Committee Approval**:
- Changing the topic or prompt of the signature assignment (as long as it still assesses WOC outcomes)
- Changing the format of the oral component (e.g., switching from a live presentation to a recorded presentation)
- Adjusting the length of the writing component within reasonable bounds (plus or minus 20%)
- Changing non-signature assignments freely

**Changes Requiring Committee Approval**:
- Modifying the signature assignment rubric criteria or performance level descriptors
- Changing the learning outcomes listed on the course's gen-ed application
- Altering the weight of the signature assignment in the final grade by more than 10 percentage points
- Removing either the writing or oral communication component
- Replacing the signature assignment with a different assignment

### QL -- Quantitative Literacy

**Full Outcome Text**: Students will demonstrate the ability to interpret and reason with quantitative information, apply mathematical or statistical methods to solve problems, and communicate quantitative arguments effectively.

**Specific Requirements**:
- Courses must include assignments requiring students to interpret quantitative data (tables, graphs, statistical summaries).
- Courses must include at least one assignment requiring students to apply mathematical or statistical methods to a real-world problem.
- Students must communicate their quantitative reasoning in writing, explaining their methods and interpreting their results in context.

**Signature Assignment**: The QL signature assignment must require students to apply quantitative methods to a problem and explain their reasoning. The rubric must include criteria for correct application of methods, interpretation of results, and communication of quantitative arguments. Submit annually with aggregate scores.

**Changes Allowed Without Committee Approval**:
- Changing the data set or problem context used in the signature assignment
- Switching between equivalent quantitative methods (e.g., using a different statistical test that assesses the same skill level)
- Adjusting computational tools (e.g., switching from Excel to R)

**Changes Requiring Committee Approval**:
- Modifying the signature assignment rubric
- Reducing the quantitative reasoning component of the course
- Changing the learning outcomes on the gen-ed application
- Altering the weight of the signature assignment significantly

### ETH -- Ethics

**Full Outcome Text**: Students will identify and analyze ethical issues and apply ethical reasoning to evaluate and respond to complex situations.

**Specific Requirements**:
- Courses must include explicit instruction in at least one ethical framework (consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics, care ethics, or discipline-specific ethical codes).
- Courses must include at least one assignment requiring students to identify ethical issues in a scenario relevant to the discipline and apply ethical reasoning to analyze the situation.
- Students must articulate their own position on an ethical question with supporting arguments.

**Signature Assignment**: The ETH signature assignment must require students to analyze an ethical scenario, apply at least one ethical framework, and articulate a reasoned position. The rubric must include criteria for issue identification, framework application, reasoning quality, and consideration of multiple perspectives. Submit annually with aggregate scores.

**Changes Allowed Without Committee Approval**:
- Changing the ethical scenario or case study used (as long as it still requires ethical analysis)
- Switching between ethical frameworks taught (as long as at least one is still included)
- Changing the format of the assignment (e.g., from an essay to a structured analysis template)

**Changes Requiring Committee Approval**:
- Modifying the signature assignment rubric
- Removing or reducing the ethics instruction component
- Changing the learning outcomes on the gen-ed application
- Reducing the ethical reasoning requirement (e.g., requiring identification of issues without analysis)

### CTCE -- Critical Thinking and Creative Expression

**Full Outcome Text**: Students will use critical and creative thinking to analyze problems, evaluate evidence, and develop innovative solutions or expressions.

**Specific Requirements**:
- Courses must include assignments that require critical analysis of sources, arguments, or creative works.
- Courses must include at least one assignment requiring students to produce an original work, design, argument, or creative expression.
- Students must demonstrate the ability to evaluate evidence or aesthetic qualities and articulate judgments with supporting reasoning.

**Signature Assignment**: The CTCE signature assignment must require students to engage in both critical analysis and creative or original production. The rubric must include criteria for analytical depth, originality, evidence evaluation, and quality of expression. Submit annually with aggregate scores.

**Changes Allowed Without Committee Approval**:
- Changing the subject matter analyzed or the creative prompt
- Switching between media or formats for the creative component (e.g., from a written essay to a multimedia presentation)
- Adjusting the balance between critical and creative components within the existing rubric framework

**Changes Requiring Committee Approval**:
- Modifying the signature assignment rubric
- Removing either the critical analysis or creative production component
- Changing the learning outcomes on the gen-ed application
- Significantly reducing the rigor of the analytical or creative requirement

## Signature Assignment Requirements

### Definition

A signature assignment is the designated assessment in a gen-ed course that serves as the primary evidence of student achievement of the gen-ed outcomes. Each gen-ed course has exactly one signature assignment. The signature assignment must:

- Directly assess the gen-ed outcomes for its category
- Use a rubric approved by the General Education committee
- Be a required component of the course (students cannot opt out)
- Carry meaningful weight in the final grade (typically at least 15%)

### Rubric Requirements

Signature assignment rubrics must:

- Include at least 3 criteria, each mapping to a specific gen-ed outcome
- Use a 4-level performance scale (typically: Exceeds, Meets, Developing, Beginning, or equivalent)
- Have descriptors that are specific enough for reliable scoring across multiple graders
- Be approved by the General Education committee before first use
- Remain consistent year-over-year unless changes are approved through the committee process

Rubric modifications -- even seemingly minor wording changes -- require committee approval because they affect the comparability of assessment data across years. The General Education assessment program depends on consistent rubrics to track student achievement trends.

### Annual Submission Process

Each semester a gen-ed course is taught, the instructor must:

1. Administer the signature assignment to all enrolled students
2. Score student work using the approved rubric
3. Submit aggregate rubric scores (distribution of students at each performance level for each criterion) to the General Education assessment office
4. Retain a sample of student artifacts (typically: 3 exemplars at the Meets level, 2 at Exceeds, 2 at Developing, 1 at Beginning) for accreditation documentation
5. Submit by the deadline specified by the assessment office (typically 2 weeks after final grades are due)

Failure to submit signature assignment data can result in the course losing its gen-ed designation upon review.

## Common General Education Compliance Risks

### Reducing Oral Communication in WOC Courses

**Risk**: Faculty may reduce or eliminate oral communication components to save class time or reduce assessment burden, particularly in large-enrollment courses where scheduling individual presentations is logistically challenging.

**Why It Matters**: The WOC designation explicitly requires both written and oral communication. A course that assesses only writing does not meet the WOC criteria, regardless of how strong the writing component is.

**Mitigation Strategies**:
- Use recorded video presentations instead of live presentations to reduce scheduling burden
- Use small-group presentations or poster sessions instead of full-class presentations
- Integrate oral communication into existing activities (e.g., code review discussions, lab presentations) rather than adding separate presentation assignments
- Never eliminate the oral component entirely; instead, find more efficient formats

### Eliminating Ethics Components

**Risk**: In technically-focused courses (e.g., CS courses with an ETH designation), faculty may deprioritize ethics content to make room for additional technical material, especially when new technical topics are added to the curriculum.

**Why It Matters**: The ETH designation requires explicit ethics instruction and assessed ethical reasoning. Reducing ethics to a single brief lecture without assessed application does not meet the criteria.

**Mitigation Strategies**:
- Embed ethics discussions into technical topics rather than treating ethics as a separate unit (e.g., discuss privacy implications when teaching database design, discuss algorithmic bias when teaching machine learning)
- Use case studies that integrate technical and ethical analysis so ethics does not feel like a separate burden
- Maintain the ethics component of the signature assignment even when restructuring other course content

### Reducing Rigor of Signature Assignments

**Risk**: Faculty may simplify signature assignments to reduce grading burden, improve student success rates, or accommodate new course structures. Simplification can take the form of reducing the scope of the assignment, lowering the cognitive demand, or making the rubric less discriminating.

**Why It Matters**: The General Education program tracks student achievement over time using signature assignment data. If the assignment becomes less rigorous, apparent improvement in scores reflects reduced expectations rather than genuine improvement in student learning. This undermines the assessment program and can be flagged during external reviews.

**Mitigation Strategies**:
- Maintain the cognitive level of the signature assignment even when other course components change
- If the assignment format needs to change, ensure the new format assesses the same outcomes at the same depth
- Seek committee approval for any substantive changes rather than making incremental reductions that accumulate over time
- Use scaffolding (drafts, checkpoints, peer review) to support student success without reducing the final product expectations

### Misunderstanding the Approval Boundary

**Risk**: Faculty may not realize which changes require committee approval and which do not, leading to either unauthorized modifications or unnecessary approval requests that slow course improvement.

**Why It Matters**: Unauthorized changes to signature assignments or rubrics can invalidate assessment data. Conversely, an overly cautious interpretation that requires approval for every minor change discourages course improvement.

**Clarifying Principle**: The boundary is between changes that affect what is assessed and how it is measured (require approval) versus changes that affect the context or topic through which it is assessed (do not require approval). Changing the ethical scenario in an ETH assignment is fine (topic change). Changing the rubric criteria for the ETH assignment requires approval (measurement change). Changing from a live presentation to a recorded presentation in a WOC course is fine (format change within oral communication). Removing the oral presentation entirely requires approval (outcome change).

When in doubt, consult the General Education committee chair before implementing changes. It is better to ask and be told approval is unnecessary than to make a change that invalidates a semester of assessment data.
