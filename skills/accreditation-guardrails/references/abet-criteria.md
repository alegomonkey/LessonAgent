# ABET Computing Accreditation Commission Criteria Reference

This reference documents the ABET CAC criteria relevant to the accreditation-guardrails skill. Use this when checking proposed course changes against accreditation requirements for computing programs.

---

## Student Outcomes (SO-1 through SO-6)

ABET CAC requires that programs enable students to attain the following outcomes by the time of graduation. Each outcome must be assessed using direct measures, and evidence of student achievement must be documented.

### SO-1: Analyze a complex computing problem and apply principles of computing and other relevant disciplines to identify solutions.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can break down a multi-faceted computing problem, identify the relevant principles (algorithmic, mathematical, domain-specific), and propose one or more viable solutions with justification.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Exams with analysis questions, design projects with problem analysis components, case study analyses, technical design documents with alternatives analysis.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year, with scored student artifacts and aggregate performance data. Rubrics must map to the outcome. Assessment must require analysis, not just recall.

### SO-2: Design, implement, and evaluate a computing-based solution to meet a given set of computing requirements in the context of the program's discipline.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can produce a working solution to a specified problem, making and justifying design decisions, implementing the solution in an appropriate language/framework, and evaluating the solution against the original requirements.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Programming projects with design documentation, capstone projects, system design and implementation assignments, lab exercises requiring design choices.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year. Artifacts must include both the implemented solution and documentation of the design process. Evaluation of the solution against requirements must be explicit.

### SO-3: Communicate effectively in a variety of professional contexts.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can produce clear written documents (reports, design documents, documentation) and deliver effective oral presentations on technical topics to both technical and non-technical audiences.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Written reports, technical documentation, oral presentations, poster sessions, code review presentations, project demos with Q&A.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year. Both written and oral communication should be assessed across the program, though not necessarily in every course. Rubrics must include communication quality criteria.

### SO-4: Recognize professional responsibilities and make informed judgments in computing practice based on legal and ethical principles.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can identify ethical issues in computing scenarios, apply ethical frameworks to analyze situations, and articulate professional responsibilities including privacy, security, accessibility, and societal impact.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Ethics case study analyses, policy analysis papers, reflective essays on professional responsibility, embedded ethics questions in technical assignments.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year. Assessment must require students to engage with ethical reasoning, not merely identify that an ethical issue exists.

### SO-5: Function effectively as a member or leader of a team engaged in activities appropriate to the program's discipline.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can contribute productively to a team, take on leadership roles when appropriate, manage conflicts, coordinate work across team members, and deliver results collaboratively.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Group projects with individual contribution tracking, peer evaluations, team-based labs, collaborative design exercises, team presentations.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year. Documentation must include evidence of individual contribution within the team context. Peer evaluation data is commonly used as supplementary evidence.

### SO-6: Apply computer science theory and software development fundamentals to produce computing-based solutions.

- **Evidence of Achievement**: Students can apply theoretical concepts (algorithms, data structures, formal languages, computational complexity) and software engineering principles (design patterns, testing, version control, documentation) to build computing solutions.
- **Typical Assessment Types**: Programming assignments, algorithm implementation projects, software engineering projects with testing and documentation requirements, technical interviews or oral exams on theoretical concepts.
- **Minimum Documentation**: At least one direct measure per academic year. Artifacts must demonstrate application of theory, not just recall. Implementation must follow software development best practices.

## Curriculum Requirements for Computer Science Programs

ABET CAC specifies the following curriculum requirements for programs granting the title "computer science":

### Required Topic Areas

The curriculum must include coverage of all the following topic areas. Topics may be distributed across multiple courses, but each must appear explicitly in at least one required course's syllabus:

- Algorithms and complexity
- Computer architecture and organization
- Data structures
- Database management (or equivalent structured data management)
- Discrete mathematics
- Human-computer interaction
- Information assurance and security
- Networking and communication
- **Operating systems** (explicitly named; cannot be omitted or treated as optional)
- Programming languages (concepts, not just one language)
- Software engineering
- Social and ethical issues in computing

### Minimum Credit Hours

The program must include at least 40 semester credit hours of computing coursework, with at least 15 credit hours at the upper division (300-level or above, or equivalent). Mathematics requirements must include discrete mathematics and must have mathematical rigor consistent with the computing content.

### Design Experience Requirement

The curriculum must include a major design experience that builds on knowledge from earlier coursework. This is typically satisfied by a capstone course or senior project. The design experience must incorporate appropriate engineering standards and multiple realistic constraints. It must require students to make design decisions and evaluate trade-offs.

### Science Requirement

The program must include at least one year (two semesters or equivalent) of a laboratory science sequence. The science courses must include laboratory components.

## Assessment Requirements

### Direct Measures

ABET requires direct measures of student achievement for each Student Outcome. A direct measure is an assessment where student performance is evaluated against defined criteria -- exams, projects, presentations graded with rubrics, or similar. Indirect measures (surveys, course evaluations, graduation rates) are supplementary but cannot replace direct measures.

### Annual Reporting

Programs must collect assessment data on a regular cycle (typically annual). Each Student Outcome must be assessed at least once per cycle. The assessment results must be reviewed by the program faculty, and actions taken in response to the results must be documented (continuous improvement process).

### Documentation for Visits

For accreditation visits, the program must provide:
- Curriculum maps showing which courses address which Student Outcomes
- Assessment plans identifying the instruments used for each outcome
- Collected student artifacts (samples of student work at multiple performance levels)
- Rubrics used for assessment, with evidence of inter-rater reliability where applicable
- Evidence of the continuous improvement process: how assessment results led to changes, and whether those changes improved outcomes
- Syllabi for all required courses, showing topic coverage and learning outcome alignment

### Continuous Improvement

ABET emphasizes that assessment is a means to continuous improvement, not an end in itself. Programs must demonstrate that they use assessment data to identify weaknesses and implement changes. The accreditation review evaluates the quality of the improvement process, not just the assessment data.

## Common Accreditation Risks

### Outcome Coverage Gaps

The most common risk is a gap in outcome coverage -- a Student Outcome that is not adequately assessed across the program. This occurs when:
- A course that was the primary assessment point for an outcome changes its assessments without coordination
- Faculty turnover leads to loss of institutional knowledge about which courses assess which outcomes
- Curriculum revisions inadvertently remove an outcome's coverage

**Mitigation**: Maintain an up-to-date curriculum map and review it whenever course changes are proposed. The accreditation-guardrails skill automates this check.

### Insufficient Assessment Instruments

Programs must have at least one direct measure for each outcome per assessment cycle. Risk arises when:
- An assessment instrument is modified or removed without updating the assessment plan
- The instrument is present but the rubric does not clearly map to the student outcome
- The instrument assesses the outcome at too low a Bloom's level (e.g., recall instead of analysis)

**Mitigation**: Verify that each assessment instrument has a rubric with explicit outcome mapping and that the assessment tasks require the cognitive level appropriate to the outcome.

### Inadequate Documentation of Changes

Accreditation bodies want to see a paper trail showing that changes were deliberate and data-driven. Risk arises when:
- Changes are made informally without documentation
- Assessment data is collected but never reviewed or acted upon
- Actions taken in response to assessment data are not recorded

**Mitigation**: Document every course change that affects accreditation-mapped components. Include the rationale, the expected impact, and a plan to assess whether the change achieved its goal.

### Contact Hour Shortfalls

Risk arises when pedagogical innovations reduce structured instructional time below the credit hour minimum. Common scenarios:
- Replacing lectures with "flipped classroom" without maintaining equivalent structured activity time
- Reducing lab hours to accommodate project work time
- Converting synchronous sessions to unstructured asynchronous work

**Mitigation**: Verify that total structured instructional time (lecture + lab + supervised project time + structured online activities) meets or exceeds the institution's credit hour policy.

### Core Topic Omission

Risk arises when curriculum changes remove required topic areas. Particular attention is warranted for:
- **Operating systems**: Explicitly named in ABET CAC criteria for CS programs. Cannot be omitted or treated as an elective topic. Must appear in at least one required course.
- **Social and ethical issues**: Sometimes deprioritized in technically focused programs but explicitly required.
- **Information assurance and security**: Relatively recent addition to requirements; some older curricula may have gaps.

**Mitigation**: Before approving any content change, verify against the required topic list that all topics remain represented in the required curriculum.
